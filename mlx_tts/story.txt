The Last Lighthouse Keeper

The storm arrived without warning on a Tuesday night.

Daniel had been the keeper of Blackrock Lighthouse for eleven years. He knew every creak of its iron stairs, every rattle of its lantern room windows. But tonight, the sea sounded different — not angry, just... hungry.

At two in the morning, his radio crackled to life.

"Blackrock Station, this is the cargo vessel Meridian. We've lost our GPS. Repeat — we have lost navigation. Do you copy?"

Daniel grabbed the handset. "Meridian, I copy you loud and clear. I'm here. Follow my light."

There was silence. Then static. Then a voice, much quieter: "We can't see you."

Daniel looked out the window. The fog had swallowed the beam whole.

He had one option left — an old foghorn, hand-cranked, that hadn't been used since 1987. The manual said it took two men to operate. Daniel was alone.

He cranked it anyway.

His arms burned. His back screamed. But every thirty seconds, a deep, resonant groan rolled out across the water — a sound so low it didn't just travel through the air, it traveled through the chest.

At four-seventeen in the morning, the Meridian's hull scraped safely into harbor.

The captain found Daniel sitting on the floor of the lighthouse, hands shaking, a cup of cold tea beside him.

"You saved twenty-six lives tonight," the captain said.

Daniel looked up and gave the only reply a lighthouse keeper could give.

"That's what the light is for."
